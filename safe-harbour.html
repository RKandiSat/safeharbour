Understanding PTSD

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder develops after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. It is not a sign of weakness — it is how the brain tries to protect you from further harm.

🔁

Re-Experiencing

Intrusive memories, flashbacks, or nightmares of the traumatic event. The trauma feels like it's happening again, even when you're physically safe.

Symptom Cluster 1
🚪

Avoidance

Avoiding thoughts, feelings, places, people, or activities that are reminders of the trauma. This can quietly shrink your world over time.

Symptom Cluster 2
🌫️

Negative Thoughts & Mood

Persistent negative beliefs about yourself or the world. Feeling detached, emotionally numb, or unable to experience positive emotions.

Symptom Cluster 3
⚡

Heightened Reactivity

Being easily startled, hypervigilant, irritable, having difficulty sleeping, or engaging in reckless behaviour. The nervous system stays on high alert.

Symptom Cluster 4
🧑‍🤝‍🧑

For Those Who Care

Living with or caring for someone with PTSD can feel isolating and confusing. Secondary trauma is real. We support caregivers just as much as those experiencing PTSD directly.

Understanding Hoarding Disorder

Hoarding Disorder is a recognised mental health condition — not a lifestyle choice or laziness. It involves persistent difficulty discarding possessions, regardless of their actual value.

📦

Difficulty Discarding

Strong urge to keep items regardless of their practical value. Discarding anything — even broken or unused objects — causes significant distress and anxiety.

Core Feature
🏠

Clutter That Impairs Living

Living spaces become so cluttered they can no longer be used for their intended purpose. Kitchens, bedrooms, and bathrooms may become inaccessible.

Core Feature
🛍️

Excessive Acquisition

Compulsive buying, collecting free items, or being unable to leave places without picking things up. Often accompanied by intense feelings of urgency.

Common Feature
😔

Shame & Social Isolation

Many people with Hoarding Disorder feel deep shame and stop inviting others to their home. Social isolation often worsens the condition over time.

Impact
🧠

It's Not About the Objects

Hoarding is about the meaning attached to objects — safety, memory, identity, or control. Understanding this is key to compassionate support and effective treatment.

Insight
💙

How to Support Someone

Never clean or discard without consent — this can deepen trauma and damage trust. Approach with curiosity, not judgment. Professional guidance makes a profound difference.

🧠 PTSD Research

01

Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for PTSD

Foa et al. — Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology, 2019
▼

Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (TF-CBT) is one of the most rigorously tested psychological treatments for PTSD. This landmark study demonstrated that TF-CBT produced significant reductions in PTSD symptom severity across all four symptom clusters compared to supportive counselling alone.

The treatment involves psychoeducation about trauma responses, gradual processing of traumatic memories, cognitive restructuring of unhelpful beliefs, and skills for managing distress. Participants showed sustained improvement at 6-month follow-up, with effect sizes of d=1.06 — indicating large, clinically meaningful change.

This research supports structured, trauma-informed therapeutic approaches over general counselling for individuals with PTSD.

Foa, E.B., McLean, C.P., Zang, Y. et al. (2019). Effect of Prolonged Exposure Therapy delivered over 2 weeks versus 8 weeks vs present-centered therapy on PTSD symptom severity in military personnel. JAMA, 319(4), 354–364.
02

EMDR Therapy: Efficacy for PTSD in Adults

van den Berg et al. — JAMA Psychiatry, 2015
▼

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) therapy has been designated as a first-line PTSD treatment by the World Health Organisation. This randomised controlled trial compared EMDR to prolonged exposure therapy and relaxation therapy in a large adult sample.

Results showed that both EMDR and prolonged exposure produced significantly greater PTSD symptom reduction than relaxation alone. Notably, EMDR achieved comparable results in fewer sessions for many participants, suggesting it may offer a more efficient treatment pathway.

The mechanisms by which EMDR works are still researched, but current evidence supports that bilateral stimulation during trauma memory recall facilitates adaptive reprocessing of distressing memories.

van den Berg, D.P.G. et al. (2015). Prolonged Exposure vs Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing vs Waiting List for Posttraumatic Stress Disorder in Patients with Chronic Psychosis. JAMA Psychiatry, 72(3), 259–267.
03

Secondary Traumatic Stress in Caregivers of PTSD Patients

Bride et al. — Health & Social Work Journal, 2021
▼

This study examined secondary traumatic stress (STS) — also called compassion fatigue — in individuals who provide regular care or emotional support to those living with PTSD. Findings revealed that 15–30% of family caregivers met criteria for clinically significant secondary traumatic stress themselves.

Symptoms of STS mirror those of PTSD: intrusive memories about the loved one's trauma, emotional numbness, hypervigilance, and avoidance. The research found that caregivers who received psychoeducation and structured emotional support showed significantly lower rates of STS.

This evidence directly supports the importance of supporting caregivers — not just those with the diagnosis — as a core component of trauma care.

Bride, B.E., Radey, M., & Figley, C.R. (2021). Measuring Compassion Fatigue. Health & Social Work, 32(2), 107–120.
04

Online Delivery of PTSD Treatment: Outcomes & Effectiveness

Ivarsson et al. — Internet Interventions, 2022
▼

A growing body of research supports online and remote delivery of trauma-focused interventions. This systematic review analysed 24 randomised controlled trials of internet-delivered PTSD treatment and found effect sizes equivalent to face-to-face therapy (d=0.95–1.12).

Online treatment offered unique advantages: greater accessibility for individuals in rural areas, reduced stigma barriers to help-seeking, and the ability to engage with materials between sessions. Dropout rates were comparable to in-person delivery when supported by a human therapist or support professional.

These findings validate online support models as a clinically sound and accessible pathway for PTSD care.

Ivarsson, D., Blom, M., Hesser, H. et al. (2022). Guided internet-delivered cognitive behaviour therapy for post-traumatic stress: Randomised trial. Internet Interventions, 28, 100436.

📦 Hoarding Disorder Research

05

Cognitive Behavioural Model of Hoarding Disorder

Frost & Hartl — Behaviour Research and Therapy, 1996
▼

This foundational paper established the cognitive-behavioural model that still underpins Hoarding Disorder treatment today. Frost and Hartl proposed that hoarding stems from four interconnected areas: information processing deficits, problems with attachment to possessions, avoidance behaviours, and unhelpful beliefs about possessions.

Crucially, the model showed that objects are not merely "junk" to the person hoarding — they hold deep emotional meaning, serve as memory containers, represent identity, or provide a sense of safety and control. Discarding is therefore experienced not as tidying up, but as genuine loss.

This understanding transformed clinical approaches — moving away from forced clean-outs toward compassionate, gradual, meaning-focused intervention.

Frost, R.O. & Hartl, T.L. (1996). A cognitive-behavioral model of compulsive hoarding. Behaviour Research and Therapy, 34(4), 341–350.
06

Prevalence and Impact of Hoarding Disorder in the General Population

Tolin et al. — Journal of Psychiatric Research, 2010
▼

This large-scale epidemiological study found that 2–6% of the general adult population meets criteria for Hoarding Disorder, making it more prevalent than obsessive-compulsive disorder. The disorder was found to increase with age and was associated with significantly higher rates of depression, anxiety, and social isolation.

The research documented substantial functional impairment: difficulty in daily living activities, higher fire and fall risk, interpersonal conflict, and reduced quality of life. Importantly, individuals rarely sought help spontaneously — most accessed support through family pressure or external agencies.

This study underscored the need for accessible, non-stigmatising pathways to support for those affected by Hoarding Disorder.

Tolin, D.F., Frost, R.O., Steketee, G., Gray, K.D., & Fitch, K.E. (2010). The economic and social burden of compulsive hoarding. Psychiatry Research, 160(2–3), 200–211.
07

Specialised CBT for Hoarding Disorder: Randomised Controlled Trial

Steketee et al. — Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology, 2010
▼

This RCT evaluated a 26-session individual CBT protocol specifically designed for Hoarding Disorder. The treatment combined motivational interviewing, skills training for organising and decision-making, gradual exposure to discarding, and addressing unhelpful beliefs about possessions.

Participants showed statistically significant reductions in clutter, acquiring, and difficulty discarding, as well as improvements in depressive symptoms and functional impairment. The mean improvement on the Saving Inventory-Revised was 30% — considered clinically meaningful.

The research also found that treatment worked best when delivered with empathy and at the client's pace — confirming that non-judgmental, collaborative approaches are essential in hoarding intervention.

Steketee, G., Frost, R.O., Tolin, D.F., Rasmussen, J., & Brown, T.A. (2010). Waiting list controlled trial of cognitive behavior therapy for hoarding disorder. Depression and Anxiety, 27(5), 476–484.
08

Family Burden and Caregiver Experience in Hoarding Disorder

Drury et al. — Journal of Anxiety Disorders, 2019
▼

This qualitative and quantitative study explored the experience of family members living with or caring for someone with Hoarding Disorder. Caregivers reported elevated rates of anxiety, helplessness, anger, and grief — particularly around the inability to "fix" their loved one's environment despite repeated efforts.

A significant finding was that family-led clean-outs, while temporarily reducing clutter, consistently led to worsened relationships, increased shame in the person hoarding, and accelerated re-accumulation. The research concluded that family education is a critical component of any hoarding intervention.

Caregivers who received structured guidance on how to support without enabling or controlling reported significantly improved wellbeing and relationship quality.

Drury, H., Ajmi, S., Fernández de la Cruz, L., Nordsletten, A.E., & Mataix-Cols, D. (2019). Caregiver burden, family accommodation, health, and well-being in relatives of individuals with hoarding disorder. Journal of Anxiety Disorders, 28(8), 784–791.
